About

I grew up in the Bronx and came to Stanford on a pre-med track. Over time I realized I was most engaged when I was building something: debugging a pipeline, writing a query, making a messy dataset legible. That pulled me into Biomedical Computation, where I get to sit at the overlap of systems, data, and human behavior.

Today that shows up in a few places: two summers as a software engineering intern on Amazon’s AGI team, working on infrastructure for Nova model customization and job-orchestration systems; ongoing research in Stanford’s Scaffolding of Cognition Lab, where I work on gaze-coding and behavioral data pipelines for developmental cognition studies; and coursework and student leadership that keep me close to questions of equity, governance, and how technology actually affects people.

The policy side of that isn’t an add-on for me, it’s the part that makes the technical work worth doing. I’m especially interested in AI governance: how models get evaluated, who gets to set the rules for customization and deployment, and what accountability looks like once a system is actually in use. Coursework like CS 121 (Equity and Governance for Artificial Intelligence) and BIOE 131 (Ethics in Bioengineering), plus watching how litigation and public data intersect in my Legal Risk Index project, has pushed me toward wanting to work on the governance and public-interest side of health, AI, and education technology, not just the engineering side of it.

I’m drawn to work where technical rigor and public consequence both matter: AI governance, health, education, and the kind of research and public-interest technology that has to hold up under real scrutiny.
