About

I study Biomedical Computation at Stanford, where my work moves between cognitive science research, software systems, and questions about how technology shapes public life.

I grew up in a Dominican family in the Bronx and came to Stanford on a pre-med track. I ended up happiest when I was building or investigating something, and the questions that stayed with me were rarely just technical. Who is this system for? Who checks it? What happens when it fails someone? Those questions run through everything here, from developmental cognition research to AI infrastructure to campus political programming.

That is why I am as interested in policy and legal work as I am in engineering. I want to work on the rules and institutions around technology, in health, education, AI, and the public interest, and the technical background is what lets me do that credibly. The short version: I use research and computational tools to understand people, systems, and decisions, and I care about who those decisions land on.

Research · Tech

Gaze & CounterCuing Analysis Pipeline

Python · Eye-tracking · Data validation

  • A research analysis workflow for processing gaze and behavioral data from developmental cognition experiments.
  • Diagnosed and fixed FPS and movie-length mismatches that were silently corrupting gaze-to-stimulus alignment, and adapted scripts across experiment versions.
  • Built validation checks comparing automated (OWLET) output against human-coded 2×2 Left/Right categorizations, so the lab can trust what the numbers say.

Policy · Tech

Legal Risk Index

SQL · BigQuery · Public data · Course project

  • An exploration of how legal and regulatory risk can be structured and scored from public data, not a deployed legal product.
  • Queried millions of rows of litigation and financial data across firms and years, aggregating early and indexing joins to keep queries fast.
  • Built and visualized a simple risk signal linking litigation history to firm fundamentals, the kind of accountability-oriented data work that pulls me toward public-interest tech.

Research

Cognitive science with real behavioral data: how young children look, learn, and build strategies, and the analysis workflows that make those findings trustworthy.

Research Assistant

Scaffolding of Cognition Lab, Stanford Psychology (PI: Cameron Ellis) · Nov. 2023 – Present

  • Long-running role in developmental cognition research, working on gaze categorization and CounterCuing analysis of how young children’s looking behavior reflects developing cognitive strategies.
  • Grew from manual behavioral coding into computational workflows: validating automated eye-tracking output (OWLET) against human-coded 2×2 Left/Right categorizations.
  • Debug and adapt analysis scripts across experiment versions, fixing data-alignment issues (FPS mismatches, stimulus-timing offsets) before they corrupt downstream results.
  • Turn messy participant data into validated, analysis-ready outputs that both researchers and code can rely on.

Policy & Public Interest

The policy side is not an add-on. It is the part that makes the technical work worth doing. I am most interested in AI governance: how models get evaluated, who sets the rules for deployment, and what accountability looks like once a system is in use. That interest is grounded in coursework on AI equity and bioethics, in the history of institutions and inequality, and in the communities I come from and have helped run.
